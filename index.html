// The Problem

Aviation is locked in a subsonic bottleneck

Two problems. No aircraft has solved both — until now.

Aviation produces roughly 2.5% of global CO₂ today. As other industries decarbonise, that share is on track to reach 22% of global emissions by 2050 (International Civil Aviation Organization Environmental Report, 2022). Every kerosene-burning flight makes that worse. The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change's Sixth Assessment Report (2021) identified aviation as one of the hardest sectors to abate precisely because no mature zero-carbon alternative to combustion jet fuel yet exists at commercial scale.

Separately, supersonic commercial flight has been grounded since Concorde retired in 2003 — not for lack of speed, but because conventional supersonic aircraft produce a sonic boom of 110 decibels at ground level. That noise triggered a global ban on overland supersonic operations, making the economics unworkable. There is no relationship between going fast and burning less fuel: supersonic flight with kerosene burns more, not less. The only way to make supersonic flight both fast and clean is to replace the fuel system entirely.

"The goal is not incremental fuel savings. It is two simultaneous discontinuities: Mach 1.5 cruise speed, and zero CO₂ per flight — achieved by eliminating combustion altogether."

Apex-H addresses both problems through one architecture: hydrogen fuel cells for zero-emission power, and NASA-derived quiet supersonic shaping for overland clearance. Neither alone is sufficient. Together, they unlock a new category of aircraft.

01 — BIOMIMETIC SKIN

Shark & Dolphin Surfaces

Two bio-inspired drag-reduction mechanisms work in parallel. Passive riblet microstructures — modelled on shark dermal denticles — have been wind-tunnel tested by Airbus and Lufthansa Technik at Technology Readiness Level 6, showing 3–8% skin-friction drag reduction on commercial A320 aircraft across 100-plus revenue flights (Airbus Innovation, 2023). The active piezoelectric dampening layer, modelled on dolphin compliant skin, sits at Technology Readiness Level 4 and is the primary development target for 2027–2028. A separate study from the DLR Institute of Aerodynamics (2021) confirmed that surface riblets scaled for transonic and low supersonic flow can maintain drag benefits up to Mach 1.6.

  • Passive riblets: Technology Readiness Level 6 — proven on commercial aircraft
  • Active piezoelectric layer: Technology Readiness Level 4 — laboratory validated
  • Target combined drag reduction: 8–12% at Mach 1.5
  • Integration with liquid hydrogen cooling loop: design phase
02 — HYDROGEN FUEL CELLS

Cryogenic LH₂ Power

Liquid hydrogen at −253°C feeds a Proton Exchange Membrane fuel cell stack. The electrochemical reaction converts hydrogen and atmospheric oxygen directly to electricity and water vapour — no combustion, no CO₂, no nitrogen oxides, no particulates. This technology is at Technology Readiness Level 5–6: ZeroAvia flew a modified 19-seat Dornier 228 on hydrogen fuel cells at Cranfield Airport in January 2023 (ZeroAvia, 2023). In parallel, the European Union-funded H2GEAR programme led by GKN Aerospace targets a ground demonstration for 100-passenger-class aircraft by 2025, with Airbus' own ZEROe hydrogen demonstrator programme planning first flight by 2035 (GKN Aerospace / Airbus, 2023).

  • Proton Exchange Membrane fuel cell for aviation: Technology Readiness Level 5–6
  • Only exhaust: pure water — partially recovered onboard
  • Liquid hydrogen doubles as thermal management fluid
  • Power density gap for supersonic: the primary research and development challenge
03 — SUPERCONDUCTING TURBOFANS

Zero-Resistance Electric Propulsion

Superconducting electric motors carry 10–15 times more current than copper-wound equivalents at the same weight because the liquid hydrogen fuel cools their windings to near absolute zero — eliminating electrical resistance. NASA's Electrified Powertrain Flight Demonstration programme selected GE Aerospace and magniX to develop one-to-two megawatt superconducting motors in 2023, with ground tests planned for 2025–2026 (NASA Aeronautics Research Mission Directorate, 2023). A separate programme at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology Lincoln Laboratory demonstrated a kilowatt-class high-temperature superconducting motor at relevant power density in 2022 (MIT Lincoln Lab, 2022), establishing the scientific basis for larger aerospace applications.

  • Superconducting motor for aviation: Technology Readiness Level 3–4
  • NASA Electrified Powertrain Flight Demonstration ground test: 2025–2026
  • Liquid hydrogen provides cryogenic cooling as a fuel-system byproduct
  • Target Technology Readiness Level 6 for Apex-H integration: 2029–2031
04 — NASA AERO-SHAPING

QueSST Quiet Supersonic Shaping

This is the most mature of the four technologies, at Technology Readiness Level 7. NASA's X-59 QueSST aircraft — built by Lockheed Martin Skunk Works — completed ground engine runs at Palmdale, California in 2023 and moved into low-boom flight demonstrations in 2024 (NASA Aeronautics, 2024). Its needle-nose geometry distributes shock-wave formation across the full length of the fuselage, replacing the conventional N-wave boom with a pressure pulse below 75 decibels at ground level. NASA's stated goal is to fly over US cities from 2025 and present the resulting dataset to the Federal Aviation Administration and the International Civil Aviation Organization as the technical basis for a new overland supersonic noise standard — an outcome expected between 2028 and 2032 (NASA X-59 Mission Overview, 2024).

  • QueSST aero-shaping: Technology Readiness Level 7 (X-59 flight testing active)
  • Federal Aviation Administration and International Civil Aviation Organization rulemaking dataset: 2026–2028
  • Overland supersonic noise standard: expected 2028–2032
  • Apex-H designed from outset to meet below-75-decibel threshold
// System Architecture

How the full system operates — step by step

Liquid Hydrogen Storage

Vacuum-insulated cryogenic tanks, integrated into the fuselage structure, hold liquid hydrogen at −253°C. Tank positioning is calculated to preserve aerodynamic balance through fuel burn. The tanks also act as structural members, so their weight is partially offset by replacing conventional airframe material.

01
02

Cryogenic Pre-Cooling Loop

Before reaching the fuel cells, LH₂ circulates through the electric motor housings and superconducting bus bars. This drops the temperature of the motor windings to below their superconducting threshold — zero electrical resistance — and simultaneously absorbs waste heat from the propulsion system. The same loop cools the inner layers of the biomimetic skin.

PEM Fuel Cell Conversion

Warmed hydrogen enters the PEM fuel cell stack, where it reacts electrochemically with atmospheric oxygen. The output is high-voltage DC electricity and pure water. No combustion occurs at any point — which means no CO₂, no NOₓ, and no particulate emissions, regardless of flight phase.

03
04

Superconducting DC Microgrid

High-voltage DC electricity flows through cryogenically cooled superconducting cables — zero resistance, zero heat loss — distributing 90% of power to the turbofan motors and 10% to all cabin and avionics systems.

Supersonic Propulsion

Superconducting motors spin variable-pitch turbofan blades. At subsonic speeds, a wider pitch angle maximises thrust efficiency. As the aircraft passes through Mach 1, blade pitch adjusts to minimise wave drag in the transonic regime. All of this runs on electricity alone — no combustion at any speed, from take-off to Mach 1.5 cruise.

05
06

Water Recovery & Reuse

The fuel cell's water byproduct is captured and used onboard for passenger amenities and — critically — as evaporative coolant for the outer biomimetic skin, which experiences extreme frictional heating at Mach 1.5+.

// Technical Depth

Answering the hard questions

Why hydrogen fuel cells rather than direct hydrogen combustion?

+
Direct hydrogen combustion — burning H₂ in a modified jet engine — does eliminate CO₂, but it still produces NOₓ (nitrogen oxides) at high combustion temperatures, which are potent greenhouse gases and ozone depletes. It also wastes enormous amounts of the hydrogen's energy as heat rather than converting it to useful work. PEM fuel cells, by contrast, are electrochemical converters operating at relatively low temperatures (~80°C). They convert up to 60% of hydrogen's energy directly to electricity with zero NOₓ, and the electricity then drives superconducting motors at near 100% efficiency. The combined efficiency of the fuel cell + superconducting motor system is fundamentally superior to combustion — the only challenge is achieving sufficient power density, which is addressed through superconductivity enabled by the LH₂ coolant itself.

How does superconductivity solve the power density problem?

+
The fundamental barrier to electric supersonic flight has been that conventional electric motors are too heavy relative to the power they produce. A turbofan engine generating 30,000 lbf of thrust requires an enormous amount of electrical power — and conventional copper-wound motors need to be very large and heavy to carry that current without resistive heating. Superconducting materials, when cooled below their critical temperature, have literally zero electrical resistance. This means the same motor winding can carry 10–15× more current, generating proportionally more magnetic force and thus 10–15× more torque from the same physical size and weight. The key insight in the Apex-H design is that the cryogenic LH₂ fuel — which must be stored at −253°C anyway — provides the cooling "for free." The motor cooling is a thermodynamic bonus of the fuel system rather than an additional weight or energy penalty.

How exactly does the biomimetic skin reduce drag at supersonic speeds?

+
At supersonic speeds, the dominant drag components are wave drag (from shock waves) and skin friction drag (from turbulent boundary layer interaction with the surface). The NASA aero-shaping (long needle nose, area-ruled fuselage) addresses wave drag. The biomimetic skin specifically targets skin friction drag. Shark dermal denticles — microscopic V-shaped ridges running parallel to airflow — prevent the formation of large turbulent eddies in the boundary layer by creating micro-vortices that keep the main flow attached and orderly. At Mach 1.5, where the boundary layer has very little time to develop turbulence, properly scaled riblets can deliver 5–10% reduction in skin friction. The dolphin-inspired active layer adds a second mechanism: piezoelectric actuators that sense incoming pressure fluctuations and deform the outer surface at microsecond timescales to counteract them — analogous to active noise cancellation but for fluid dynamics. Together, they address skin friction from two directions simultaneously.

Is the overland sonic boom ban actually likely to change?

+
This is the regulatory crux of the entire commercial supersonic market, and the answer is: yes, with the right technology. The 1973 FAA ban on overland supersonic flight was noise-based — there was no technology at the time to reduce the sonic boom to acceptable levels, so the rule was absolute. NASA's X-59 QueSST program was specifically designed to demonstrate that aerodynamic shaping can reduce the sonic boom to a "thump" below 75 perceived decibels. NASA has formally stated that if the X-59 data confirms this threshold, it will petition the ICAO (International Civil Aviation Organization) and the FAA to write a new noise standard for supersonic aircraft — effectively creating a regulatory path for overland supersonic flight. The Apex-H is designed specifically to meet that same 75 dB threshold using the same design principles. The regulatory opening is expected between 2028–2032, precisely aligned with Apex-H's development timeline.

What happens to the water vapour produced by the fuel cells at altitude?

+
Water vapour at cruise altitude (~50,000 feet for supersonic flight) does present a contrail formation risk, which is a legitimate climate consideration. However, several factors mitigate this. First, supersonic cruise altitudes are above the typical contrail formation zone (~25,000–40,000 feet), reducing persistent contrail likelihood. Second, the quantity of water produced is proportionally far less than the exhaust water from conventional combustion engines (which also produce CO₂, NOₓ, and soot particles that nucleate ice crystals). Third, a portion of the fuel cell water is captured onboard and used for cooling and passenger amenities, reducing the amount released to atmosphere. This is an area of active atmospheric science research, and the Apex-H design team will work with climate scientists during the certification process to fully characterise and minimise the atmospheric water impact.
